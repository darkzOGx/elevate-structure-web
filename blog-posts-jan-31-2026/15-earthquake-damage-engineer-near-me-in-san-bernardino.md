### Visible Damage After Earthquakes

**Clear Indicators**
Obvious damage requiring assessment includes:

**Warning Symptoms**

**Engineering Evaluation**

Foundation damage patterns include:

### Wall System Damage

**Vertical Element Cracking**
Wall damage commonly includes:
- Diagonal cracks at window and door corners
- X-shaped shear cracks
- Separation at wall intersections
- Stucco cracking and spalling
- Interior drywall damage

### Chimney Problems

**Common Vulnerability**
Chimneys frequently sustain damage:
- Separation from building structure
- Cracking in masonry
- Toppling or collapse
- Damage to roof where chimney penetrates
- Leaning chimneys

Chimneys are often the first element to show earthquake damage.

### Unreinforced Masonry Failures

**High-Risk Construction**
URM buildings experience:
- Wall cracking and displacement
- Parapet failures
- Wall-to-roof connection failures
- Out-of-plane wall collapse

### Soft-Story Damage

Buildings with weak ground floors show:
- Story drift and leaning

**Joint Problems**
Structural connections may fail:

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How soon after an earthquake should I get my building assessed?

For buildings with visible damage, assessment should occur as soon as safely possibleâ€”often within hours to a few days. For buildings without obvious damage, assessment within a week of a significant felt earthquake provides peace of mind and documents pre-existing condition. Delay can make it harder to attribute damage specifically to the seismic event.

### What's the difference between a building inspector and a structural engineer for earthquake assessment?

Building inspectors (from the city) can tag buildings as unsafe based on visual observation but typically cannot provide detailed engineering evaluation or repair design. Structural engineers evaluate the actual structural systems, determine damage severity with engineering analysis, and design repairs. For damaged buildings, you'll typically need bothâ€”officials for tagging/clearance and engineers for assessment/repair.

### Will insurance cover earthquake damage assessment and repair?

Standard homeowner's insurance does NOT cover earthquake damageâ€”you need separate earthquake insurance. If you have earthquake insurance, it typically covers assessment and repair costs, subject to your deductible (often 10-15% of home value). Without earthquake insurance, assessment and repairs are out-of-pocket. Assessment costs are modest compared to unaddressed structural damage risks.

### How do I know if my building needs seismic retrofitting to prevent future damage?

Post-earthquake assessment often identifies pre-existing vulnerabilities that the earthquake revealed or could worsen in future events. Common retrofit recommendations include foundation bolting, cripple wall bracing, soft-story strengthening, and chimney bracing. We can evaluate retrofit options as part of damage assessment or as a separate service.

### What if my building looks fine but I felt strong shaking?

Buildings can sustain hidden damage not visible without inspection. If you experienced strong shaking (items falling, difficulty standing), professional assessment provides valuable documentation and peace of mind. Assessment can identify connection damage, foundation movement, or framing stress that isn't visible but could progress in aftershocks or future earthquakes.

### Can you help with earthquake insurance claims?

Yes, our assessment reports are formatted to support insurance claims, with detailed damage documentation, clear damage attribution, and repair scope identification. We can coordinate with your insurance adjuster and provide supplemental documentation if claim questions arise.
