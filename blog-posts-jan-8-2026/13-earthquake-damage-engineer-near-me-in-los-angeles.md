## Frequently Asked Questions

**How soon after an earthquake should I get my building assessed?**

For significant earthquakes (felt strongly, visible damage), assessment should occur as soon as safely possibleâ€”ideally within 24-48 hours. For moderate events where you're uncertain, assessment within a week is appropriate. If the building department has tagged your building, assessment should begin immediately to start the clearance process. Don't delay if you have safety concerns.

**Should I evacuate my building after an earthquake?**

Evacuate if: there's obvious severe damage, you smell gas, the building is leaning or has collapsed areas, you see large cracks through structural elements, or you feel the building is unsafe. Stay outside until professional assessment confirms safety. For less obvious situations, if you're uncertain, err on the side of caution and have the building assessed before re-occupying.

**What's the difference between ATC-20 assessment and engineering assessment?**

ATC-20 assessment is rapid safety screening performed by trained volunteers (often engineers/architects) immediately after major earthquakes. It results in placards (green/yellow/red) indicating basic safety status. Engineering assessment is comprehensive evaluation by a licensed PE that documents specific damage, determines causes, provides repair recommendations, and produces documentation for insurance claims, permit applications, and building department requirements.

**Does my insurance cover earthquake damage assessment?**

Earthquake insurance (a separate policy from standard homeowners) typically covers reasonable engineering assessment costs as part of the claims process. Standard homeowners insurance does NOT cover earthquake damage. Review your specific policy. Keep all receiptsâ€”assessment costs are typically reimbursable under earthquake policies. We can provide documentation formatted for insurance purposes.

**What if the assessment finds my building is unsafe?**

If assessment determines your building is unsafe for occupancy: (1) you cannot occupy until repairs are made and approved, (2) the engineer will define the scope of required repairs, (3) you'll need engineered repair plans for permits, (4) construction must be performed per approved plans with inspections, (5) final approval reinstates occupancy. This process takes weeks to months depending on damage severity.

**Can earthquake damage be repaired, or does the building need to be demolished?**

Most earthquake damage can be repaired with proper engineering design. Complete demolition is rare and typically only for buildings with catastrophic damage or where repair costs exceed practical thresholds. Modern repair techniques include epoxy injection, fiber reinforcement, steel plate addition, complete element replacement, and seismic retrofit improvements. Assessment determines whether repair is practical and cost-effective.
