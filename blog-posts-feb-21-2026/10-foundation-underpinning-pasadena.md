## What Is Foundation Underpinning?

Foundation underpinning is the process of strengthening and deepening an existing foundation to reach more competent bearing soils or to increase the foundation's load-carrying capacity. In Pasadena, foundation underpinning is required when:

- **The existing foundation has settled** — Differential settlement causes structural damage, cracked walls, and unlevel floors throughout the Pasadena building
- **Soil conditions have changed** — Drought, flooding, plumbing leaks, or groundwater changes alter soil bearing capacity beneath Pasadena foundations
- **Building loads are increasing** — Second-story additions, heavy equipment installation, or change of use increases loads beyond the existing foundation's capacity
- **Adjacent excavation threatens stability** — New construction or utility installation near a Pasadena building undermines existing foundation support
- **The original foundation is inadequate** — Older Pasadena homes (pre-1960s) often have shallow, unreinforced foundations that do not meet current code standards

Foundation underpinning in Pasadena transfers building loads from shallow, inadequate bearing soils to deeper, stable soil layers or bedrock. This permanently solves settlement problems and prevents future foundation movement.

## Foundation Underpinning Methods Used in Pasadena

### Push Pier Underpinning

Push piers are the most widely used foundation underpinning method in Pasadena for existing buildings experiencing settlement.

**System Description**
Steel push piers consist of interlocking pipe sections hydraulically driven through bracket assemblies attached to the existing foundation. The piers penetrate through compressible or unstable soils until they reach competent bearing material — typically 15 to 40 feet deep in Pasadena depending on location and soil profile.

**How Push Piers Work in Pasadena**

1. **Excavation** — Small pits (approximately 3 feet square) are excavated at each pier location along the Pasadena foundation perimeter. Pier spacing is typically 5 to 8 feet based on load distribution.

2. **Bracket Installation** — A steel bracket is secured beneath the existing Pasadena foundation footing using anchor bolts or pins. The bracket serves as the pier's connection to the building.

3. **Pier Driving** — Interlocking steel pipe sections (typically 2-7/8" to 3-1/2" diameter) are hydraulically driven through the bracket into the soil. The weight of the Pasadena building serves as reaction force for pier installation. Each pier section advances until it reaches competent bearing soil or predetermined driving resistance.

4. **Load Transfer** — Once all piers reach adequate bearing depth, hydraulic jacks at each pier location simultaneously lift and transfer the Pasadena building's weight from the settling soil to the pier system.

5. **Foundation Lifting** — If specified by the structural engineer, the hydraulic jacks lift the Pasadena foundation toward its original elevation. Lifting occurs gradually in controlled increments to prevent damage to the superstructure.

6. **Lock-Off and Backfill** — Pier brackets are permanently locked, excavations are backfilled, and the site is restored.

**Advantages for Pasadena Properties**
- Reaches competent bearing layers through all problematic Pasadena soil types
- Uses the building's weight as installation reaction force (no external equipment needed)
- Immediate load transfer — no waiting for concrete to cure
- Foundation lifting capability restores floor levelness
- Minimal landscape and site disruption
- Works for all Pasadena building types: residential, commercial, and historic

### Helical Pier Underpinning

Helical piers use steel shafts with welded helical plates that are screwed into the soil using rotary equipment. They are particularly effective for Pasadena projects where:

**Lighter Structures** — Pasadena homes with lightweight construction (wood frame, single story) may not provide sufficient reaction force for push pier installation. Helical piers do not require building weight for installation — torque-driven equipment advances the piers independently.

**New Construction** — Helical piers installed before a Pasadena building is constructed provide deep bearing support from day one, preventing settlement in new homes built on challenging soils.

**Interior Locations** — Helical pier equipment can operate in confined spaces, allowing installation through basement floors or crawl spaces in Pasadena homes where exterior access is limited.

**Tension Applications** — Helical piers resist both compression and tension forces, making them ideal for applications where uplift resistance is needed, such as retaining walls and seismic tie-down anchors in Pasadena.

**Engineering Design for Pasadena**
The structural engineer specifies:
- Helix plate diameter and configuration for Pasadena soil conditions
- Shaft diameter based on load requirements and buckling analysis
- Installation torque-to-capacity correlation for quality control
- Minimum embedment depth past problematic soil layers
- Bracket type and connection to existing Pasadena foundation

### Micropile Underpinning

Micropiles (also called mini-piles) are small-diameter drilled and grouted piles used for foundation underpinning in Pasadena when:

- Piers must penetrate through rock or very dense soils
- Vibration restrictions prohibit driven piers (adjacent to sensitive Pasadena structures)
- Limited headroom prevents standard equipment operation
- Extremely high loads require engineered pile capacity

**Micropile Construction in Pasadena**
1. Drill a small-diameter hole (4 to 12 inches) through the existing foundation and into bearing soil
2. Install high-strength steel reinforcement (threaded bar or steel casing)
3. Grout the hole with structural cement grout under pressure
4. Connect the micropile to the existing foundation with a structural pile cap

Micropiles in Pasadena achieve capacities of 50 to 200+ tons per pile, supporting heavy commercial buildings and structures requiring significant load capacity increases.

### Conventional Underpinning (Mass Concrete)

Traditional underpinning extends the existing Pasadena foundation deeper by excavating beneath the footing in alternating sections and placing reinforced concrete:

1. Excavate a pit beneath a section of the existing foundation (typically 3 to 5 feet in length)
2. Deepen the excavation to competent bearing soil
3. Place reinforced concrete in the pit, contacting the underside of the existing footing
4. Allow concrete to cure before excavating adjacent sections
5. Repeat in alternating sequence along the entire Pasadena foundation

**Advantages in Pasadena**
- Proven method with a long track record
- No specialized equipment required
- Increases foundation width as well as depth
- Effective for moderate settlement where pier depths are not excessive

**Limitations**
- Slow process (concrete must cure between sections)
- Significant excavation and soil disruption
- Limited practical depth (typically 8 to 12 feet below existing footing)
- Cannot be used for foundation lifting

### Phase 2: Engineering Design

The structural engineer designs the Pasadena foundation underpinning system:

**Pier Design** — Select pier type, diameter, capacity, and spacing for the Pasadena project:
- Individual pier capacity matched to tributary building load
- Pier spacing optimized for cost-effective load distribution
- Minimum pier depth based on geotechnical boring data
- Safety factors per California Building Code requirements

**Connection Design** — Engineer the bracket or pile cap connections between underpinning elements and the existing Pasadena foundation:
- Bracket type and size for existing footing geometry
- Anchor bolt specifications for foundation attachment
- Load transfer mechanism verification
- Compatibility with existing foundation materials

**Lifting Plan** — If foundation lifting is specified:
- Maximum lift amount at each pier location
- Lifting sequence to prevent structural damage
- Monitoring points and tolerance criteria
- Contingency procedures for unexpected behavior
