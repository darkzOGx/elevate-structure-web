### What is the difference between tilt-up and steel frame warehouse construction?
Tilt-up construction uses concrete wall panels cast on-site and tilted into position, offering cost-effective enclosed structures. Steel frame construction uses structural steel columns and beams with metal panel or masonry walls, providing flexibility for future modifications and larger clear spans. Tilt-up typically costs less; steel frame offers more flexibility.

### What floor slab thickness is required for warehouse operations?
Warehouse floor slab thickness depends on specific loading requirements. Light-duty warehouses typically use 5-6 inch slabs. Standard distribution with racking requires 6-7 inch slabs. Heavy industrial or high-density racking may require 8-10+ inch slabs. Floor design considers racking loads, forklift traffic, and soil conditions.

### Do warehouse buildings require seismic engineering in Anaheim?
Yes, all buildings in Anaheim require seismic design per California Building Code. Industrial warehouses must have lateral force-resisting systems designed for the site's seismic zone. Tilt-up buildings require specific seismic detailing for panels and connections. Warehouse contents (racking, equipment) may also require seismic restraint.

### What clear height is standard for modern distribution centers?
Modern distribution centers typically require 32-40 feet clear height to accommodate high-rise racking systems and maximize storage density. E-commerce fulfillment centers increasingly demand 36-40 feet. Clear height significantly impacts structural design, particularly for tilt-up buildings where panel height and roof spans must accommodate the higher walls.

### Can existing warehouses be structurally modified for new uses?
Yes, existing warehouses can be modified for new uses including increased floor loads, mezzanine additions, increased clear height, and new door openings. Structural evaluation determines existing capacity and required upgrades. Modifications must meet current California Building Code seismic requirements, which may trigger additional retrofit work.
