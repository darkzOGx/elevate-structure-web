## What Is Office Building Structural Engineering?

Office building structural engineering encompasses the analysis, design, and documentation of load-carrying systems in commercial office buildings — from single-story professional suites to multi-story high-rise towers. The discipline addresses:

**Gravity Systems.** Floor beams, girders, and columns that carry dead loads (structural self-weight, finishes, mechanical systems) and live loads (occupants, furniture, equipment). The 2022 California Building Code specifies office live loads at 50 psf for general office use and 80–125 psf for corridors, lobbies, and mechanical rooms.

**Lateral Force Resisting Systems (LFRS).** The structural elements — moment frames, shear walls, or braced frames — that resist seismic and wind forces and prevent building collapse or unacceptable drift. In Newport Beach, ASCE 7-22 governs lateral design, with seismic parameters derived from USGS seismic hazard maps for the coastal Orange County region.

**Foundation Systems.** Spread footings, mat foundations, or deep pile foundations that transfer building loads to the soil. Newport Beach's coastal geology — including bay mud, beach sand, and alluvial deposits — frequently requires geotechnical investigation and engineered foundation solutions.

**Tenant Improvement Structural Work.** Modifications to existing office buildings that change floor loading, remove walls or columns, add mezzanine levels, or create new floor openings for stairs or atriums.

## Types of Office Building Structural Engineering Services in Newport Beach

### 1. High-Rise Office Building Structural Design

Newport Beach's Fashion Island area and MacArthur Boulevard corridor contain a significant inventory of 5- to 20-story office towers. Structural engineering for high-rise office buildings in Newport Beach involves:

**Seismic Analysis.** High-rise office buildings (buildings over 65 feet in height in SDC D) require a modal response spectrum analysis (MRSA) or nonlinear response history analysis (NRHA) per ASCE 7-22. This analysis accounts for higher-mode effects that simplified static analysis methods miss.

**Wind Engineering.** Newport Beach's coastal location places office towers in ASCE 7-22 Exposure Category D — the most demanding wind exposure category. Design wind pressures for glazing systems, cladding, and the main wind force resisting system are significantly higher than inland values.

**Special Moment Resisting Frames (SMRF).** Most Newport Beach high-rise office towers use steel SMRF as the primary lateral system, with AISC 341-22 detailing requirements for connections, column splices, and panel zone design.

**Peer Review.** California law requires independent peer review of the structural design for high-rise buildings (over 160 feet) and for buildings using performance-based design procedures. Our engineers coordinate peer review submittals and respond to peer reviewer comments efficiently.

### 2. Tenant Improvement Structural Engineering

The most common office structural engineering work in Newport Beach is tenant improvement (TI) — the structural portion of a tenant's suite build-out. Typical TI structural scope includes:

Newport Beach TI engineering fees: $5,000–$12,000 for typical suite remodels; $12,000–$20,000 for complex projects with column removal or mezzanine additions.

### 3. Seismic Evaluation and Retrofit

Newport Beach's older office building inventory — particularly buildings constructed before the 1994 Northridge earthquake — may have inadequate seismic performance. Our engineers perform:

**Seismic Evaluation per ASCE 41-17.** Tier 1 screening, Tier 2 deficiency-based evaluation, and Tier 3 nonlinear analysis to assess existing office building seismic performance against Life Safety or Immediate Occupancy performance objectives.

**Retrofit Design.** Steel moment frame addition, buckling-restrained brace (BRB) retrofit, viscous damper installation, or base isolation design for performance-based seismic upgrade.

Many Newport Beach landlords pursue voluntary seismic retrofit to improve tenant occupancy rates and reduce liability exposure. Our engineers have designed voluntary seismic retrofits for numerous Newport Beach office buildings in the Newport Center and Airport Area submarkets.

### 4. Addition and Vertical Expansion Structural Engineering

### 5. Data Center and Server Room Structural Engineering

## Newport Beach Geographic and Regulatory Considerations

### Coastal Seismic Environment

Newport Beach is located approximately 4 miles from the Newport-Inglewood Fault — one of the most historically active fault systems in Southern California, responsible for the 1933 Long Beach earthquake (magnitude 6.4). The USGS seismic hazard maps assign Newport Beach spectral acceleration values of Ss = 1.5–1.8g (0.2-second period) and S1 = 0.6–0.7g (1.0-second period). Most Newport Beach office buildings are classified in Seismic Design Category D (SDC D).

Risk Category II office buildings in SDC D must be designed for the Maximum Considered Earthquake (MCE) ground motions factored by 2/3 to obtain the design-level motions. For Risk Category III or IV office buildings (buildings with large occupancy loads or essential facilities), design to the full MCE is required.

### Coastal Wind Environment

Newport Beach's Pacific Ocean frontage places it in ASCE 7-22 Wind Exposure Category D for buildings within one mile of the coastline — the most demanding wind exposure category in the standard. Exposure D increases design wind pressures by 15–25% compared to the inland Exposure C classification. For Newport Beach high-rise office towers, wind drift controls for comfort (limiting floor-to-floor drift to H/400 to H/600) often govern the lateral system design rather than strength alone.

### Newport Beach Building Department

The Newport Beach Community Development Department — Building Division processes commercial structural permits. Key facts:

- Plan check turnaround: 4–8 weeks for over-the-counter; 2–4 weeks for electronic submittals
- Structural calculations must be signed and stamped by a California-licensed PE or SE
- The City enforces the 2022 CBC, 2022 CFC, and Newport Beach Municipal Code
- High-rise buildings (over 55 feet) are subject to enhanced fire and life safety requirements
- The City requires a soils report for any project with new footings or foundation modifications

### Newport Beach Development Community

The Irvine Company is the dominant landlord in Newport Center and much of the Airport Area submarket, with standardized design criteria for tenant improvements in its buildings. ### Soil Conditions in Newport Beach

Newport Beach's geology varies dramatically by neighborhood:

- **Newport Center/Fashion Island Area:** Primarily Quaternary alluvium with moderate bearing capacity; spread footings are typical.
- **Balboa Peninsula and Lido Isle:** Beach sand and bay mud deposits with low bearing capacity; mat foundations or driven piles are often required.
- **Airport Area (near John Wayne Airport):** Quaternary alluvium and marine terrace deposits; conditions are generally favorable for conventional foundations.
- **Corona del Mar:** Marine terrace with sandy loam; generally good bearing capacity.

## Common Structural Challenges in Newport Beach Office Buildings

### Challenge 1: Slab Penetration for Internal Stairs in Multi-Story Offices

Law firms, financial companies, and corporate tenants in Newport Beach frequently want internal connecting stairs between leased floors. Creating a stair opening in a concrete or composite steel deck floor requires structural engineering: framing the opening with doubled beams, designing a new trimmer system, verifying the existing girders and columns are not overloaded by the redistributed loads, and ensuring the diaphragm remains continuous for lateral force transfer. Our engineers have designed dozens of internal stair penetrations in Newport Beach office buildings.

### Challenge 2: Server Room Floor Strengthening

A Newport Beach financial firm adding a trading floor or data center may need floor live load capacity increased from 50 psf (standard office) to 150–250 psf (data center). Options include installing a new structural steel platform over the existing slab, adding below-slab temporary shoring during construction, or — for ground-floor locations — thickening the slab with additional reinforcement. Our engineers analyze the most cost-effective solution for each Newport Beach office building's specific framing system.

### Challenge 3: Curtain Wall and Glass Facade Modifications

Newport Beach office towers feature extensive glazed curtain wall systems that define the building's architectural character. When tenants or landlords modify the facade — adding new windows, replacing curtain wall panels, or installing new mechanical louvers — the structural engineer must verify the facade framing, anchor conditions, and wind load resistance. In Newport Beach's Exposure D wind environment, curtain wall anchor forces are higher than in typical inland applications.

### Challenge 4: Seismic Drift Compatibility in Older Buildings

Newport Beach office buildings constructed in the 1970s and 1980s were designed to pre-Northridge seismic standards with significantly lower drift limits and weaker connection detailing than current AISC 341-22 requires. When these buildings undergo significant TI work that triggers seismic compliance thresholds, our engineers assess whether the existing lateral system meets current drift performance expectations and design targeted upgrades where deficiencies exist.

### Challenge 5: Foundation Upgrades in Coastal Soils

Newport Beach's bay-adjacent office properties often sit on compressible bay mud or saturated sandy soils that require deep foundations — driven steel H-piles or drilled concrete caissons — rather than conventional spread footings. When existing offices expand or undergo heavy equipment additions, our engineers assess existing foundation capacity and design supplemental deep foundations where needed.

## Frequently Asked Questions: Office Building Structural Engineering in Newport Beach

### 1. Does a Newport Beach office tenant improvement always require a structural engineer?

Any TI that removes or modifies a load-bearing wall, adds a mezzanine, creates a new floor penetration, adds rooftop equipment, or modifies the building's structural frame requires stamped structural engineering drawings from a California-licensed PE. Purely non-structural work — recarpeting, repainting, adding non-load-bearing partitions — does not require a structural engineer, though the architect's drawings must still be permitted.

### 2. What is the typical plan check timeline for office structural permits in Newport Beach?

The Newport Beach Building Division processes structural plan checks in 4–8 weeks for over-the-counter submittals and 2–4 weeks for electronic submittals on eligible projects. AAA Engineering Design delivers complete, code-compliant structural packages that minimize correction cycles. Most of our Newport Beach office projects achieve permit issuance within 6–10 weeks of first submittal.

### 3. What ASCE 7 seismic design category applies to Newport Beach office buildings?

Most Newport Beach office buildings are Risk Category II structures assigned to Seismic Design Category D (SDC D) per ASCE 7-22, based on the City's mapped spectral acceleration values and the proximity to the Newport-Inglewood Fault. SDC D requires special structural systems (SMRF or special shear walls), special inspection, and seismic anchorage of nonstructural components.

### 4. How does coastal wind exposure affect office structural design in Newport Beach?

Newport Beach structures within one mile of the Pacific Ocean are classified as ASCE 7-22 Wind Exposure Category D. This classification increases design wind pressures by 15–25% compared to Exposure C, which affects the design of curtain wall systems, roof cladding, canopies, signage, and the main wind force resisting system. Our engineers account for Exposure D in all Newport Beach structural designs.

### 5. What does it cost to add a mezzanine level to a Newport Beach office space?

Structural engineering fees for a mezzanine addition in a Newport Beach office typically run $8,000–$15,000, covering the structural design, calculations, and permit drawings. ### 6. Does Newport Beach require a soils report for office tenant improvements?

Newport Beach requires a soils report for projects that involve new foundations, footing modifications, or below-grade construction. Standard above-slab tenant improvements in existing office buildings do not require a new soils report unless the scope triggers foundation work. Our engineers advise clients on geotechnical requirements as part of the initial project scoping.

### 7. Can a Newport Beach office building be voluntarily upgraded for better seismic performance?

Yes. Newport Beach landlords and owner-occupants pursue voluntary seismic upgrade — typically targeting an Immediate Occupancy or Life Safety performance objective per ASCE 41-17 — to reduce earthquake damage risk, lower insurance premiums, and attract tenants who prioritize business continuity. Our engineers design cost-effective retrofit systems including steel moment frames, buckling-restrained braces, and viscous dampers for Newport Beach office buildings of all vintages.

### 8. What structural systems are used in Newport Beach high-rise office towers?

Newport Beach high-rise office towers typically use one of three lateral systems: steel special moment resisting frames (SMRF), reinforced concrete special moment frames, or dual systems combining a moment frame with a special shear wall or braced frame. The choice depends on building height, floor plate geometry, architectural constraints, and cost optimization. For buildings over 160 feet, performance-based design is common.
