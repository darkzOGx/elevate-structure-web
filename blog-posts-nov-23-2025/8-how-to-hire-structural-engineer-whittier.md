## Step 1: Understand Required Qualifications

### California PE License (Non-Negotiable)

**What to Verify:**
- Active California Professional Engineer license
- Civil or Structural discipline
- Current license status (no expiration or suspension)
- No disciplinary actions

**How to Verify:**
Visit the California Board for Professional Engineers, Land Surveyors, and Geologists website (bpelsg.ca.gov) and search by name or license number. This takes 2 minutes and is absolutely essential.

**Red Flag:**
Anyone offering "engineering services" without a valid California PE license is breaking the law and putting your project at risk.

### Professional Liability Insurance

**What to Request:**
- Certificate of insurance
- Minimum $1 million coverage (preferably $2+ million)
- Current policy (not expired)
- Coverage for your project type

**Why It Matters:**
Professional liability insurance protects you if engineering errors occur. Uninsured engineers put your entire project and personal assets at risk.

### Relevant Experience

**Look For:**
- Portfolio of similar projects
- Experience with your project type (residential, commercial, industrial)
- Local Whittier/LA County experience
- Successful permit approvals
- References from past clients

**Project-Specific Experience:**
- Home additions: Look for residential addition portfolio
- Seismic retrofitting: Verify retrofit experience
- Commercial: Confirm commercial project experience
- Foundation issues: Check foundation repair background

## Step 3: Evaluate Candidates

### Initial Consultation

Most reputable Whittier structural engineers offer free initial consultations. Use this to assess:

**Technical Competence**
- Do they understand your project?
- Can they explain technical concepts clearly?
- Do they identify potential challenges?
- Do they offer preliminary solutions?

**Communication Style**
- Do they listen to your concerns?
- Do they explain things in understandable terms?
- Are they responsive to questions?
- Do they seem genuinely interested in your project?

**Local Knowledge**
- Are they familiar with Whittier building department?
- Do they know local soil conditions?
- Have they worked in your neighborhood?
- Do they understand local code amendments?

### Questions to Ask

**About Their Qualifications:**
1. What is your California PE license number? (Verify independently)
2. How long have you been practicing structural engineering?
3. What percentage of your work is residential/commercial? (Match to your project)
4. How many projects have you completed in Whittier?
5. Can you provide references from similar projects?

**About Their Process:**
6. What is your typical design timeline for projects like mine?
7. What information do you need from me to start?
8. How do you handle plan check comments and revisions?
9. Do you provide construction support? What's included?
10. How do you communicate during the project?

**About Costs:**
11. What is your fee structure? (Fixed fee, hourly, percentage)
12. What services are included in your fee?
13. What triggers additional charges?
14. What is your payment schedule?
15. Do you provide a written proposal?

**About Local Experience:**
16. How familiar are you with Whittier building department requirements?
17. What is your typical permit approval timeline in Whittier?
18. Have you worked with my contractor/architect before?
19. What challenges do you anticipate for my project?
20. How do you handle unexpected site conditions?

### Red Flags to Watch For

**Immediate Disqualifiers:**
- No valid California PE license
- Unwilling to provide license information
- No professional liability insurance
- Refuses to provide references
- Offers to stamp plans prepared by others

**Warning Signs:**
- Significantly lower fees than other qualified engineers (often indicates inexperience or cutting corners)
- Promises guaranteed permit approval timelines
- Dismisses your concerns or questions
- Poor communication or slow responses
- No local Whittier experience
- No written proposal or contract
- Vague scope of services
- Pressure to sign immediately

**Communication Red Flags:**
- Doesn't return calls or emails promptly
- Can't explain technical concepts clearly
- Talks over you or doesn't listen
- Seems rushed or disinterested
- Provides conflicting information

### What a Good Proposal Includes

**Scope of Services**
Detailed description of what the engineer will provide:
- Site visit and investigation
- Structural calculations
- Engineering drawings (foundation plans, framing plans, details)
- Signed and stamped documents
- Permit submittal assistance
- Plan check response (number of revisions included)
- Construction support (if included)

**Deliverables**
Specific items you'll receive:
- Number of plan sets
- Calculation package (if required)
- Digital files (PDF, CAD)
- Permit-ready documents

**Timeline**
- Design phase duration
- Permit submittal timing
- Expected permit approval timeframe
- Milestones and dependencies

**Fees**
- Total fee or fee structure
- Payment schedule
- Reimbursable expenses
- Additional services costs
- Revision policy

**Terms & Conditions**
- Liability limitations
- Ownership of documents
- Termination provisions
- Dispute resolution

**Evaluate Total Value:**
- Qualifications and experience
- Scope completeness
- Communication quality
- Local knowledge
- References and reputation

- Price (but not as sole factor)

## Step 7: Understand the Contract

### Essential Contract Elements

**Parties & Project**
- Your name and address
- Engineer's name, license number, and firm
- Project address and description
- Effective date

**Scope of Services**
- Detailed description of what engineer will provide
- Specific deliverables
- Number of revisions included
- Construction support provisions

**Fees & Payment**
- Total fee or fee calculation method
- Payment schedule
- Reimbursable expenses
- Additional services rates
- Late payment terms

**Timeline**
- Design phase duration
- Deliverable dates
- Dependencies and assumptions

**Responsibilities**
- What you must provide (existing plans, access, etc.)
- What engineer will provide
- Third-party coordination (architect, contractor, etc.)

**Liability & Insurance**
- Professional liability insurance confirmation
- Limitation of liability provisions
- Indemnification clauses

**Termination**
- How either party can terminate
- Notice requirements
- Payment for work completed

**Dispute Resolution**
- Mediation or arbitration provisions
- Governing law (California)

### Before You Sign

**Review Carefully:**
- Read entire contract
- Understand all terms
- Ask questions about anything unclear
- Have attorney review if complex or high-value project

**Negotiate if Needed:**
- Payment terms
- Scope clarifications
- Timeline adjustments
- Additional services

**Get Everything in Writing:**
- All agreements and changes
- Email confirmations of verbal discussions
- Signed and dated contract
