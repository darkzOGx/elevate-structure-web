## What Is Earthquake Damage Engineering?

Earthquake damage engineering involves professional assessment and repair of structures affected by seismic events. This specialized discipline addresses:

- **Safety evaluation** - Determining if buildings are safe to occupy
- **Damage assessment** - Identifying and documenting structural damage
- **Cause analysis** - Understanding why damage occurred
- **Repair design** - Engineering solutions to restore structural integrity
- **Strengthening recommendations** - Preventing future damage

## What Types of Earthquake Damage Require Engineering?

### Structural Frame Damage
Building skeleton effects:

**Steel Frame Buildings:**
- Connection fractures
- Column buckling
- Beam deformation
- Brace failures

**Concrete Frame Buildings:**
- Column shear failures
- Beam plastic hinging
- Joint damage
- Concrete spalling

**Wood Frame Buildings:**
- Cripple wall collapse
- Foundation separation
- Shear wall damage
- Connection failures

### Foundation Damage
Ground-level effects:

**Common Issues:**
- Foundation cracking
- Settlement from liquefaction
- Foundation-wall separation
- Slab heaving or cracking

**Liquefaction Damage:**
- Ground settlement
- Lateral spreading
- Building tilting
- Utility disruption

### Non-Structural Damage
Secondary effects that may indicate structural issues:

**Indicators:**
- Chimney damage or collapse
- Parapet failures
- Cladding or veneer damage
- Interior finish cracking

### Soft-Story Collapse
Buildings with weak first floors:

**High-Risk Buildings:**
- Tuck-under parking
- Large ground-floor openings
- Multiple stories above open floor
- Pre-1978 wood construction

**Failure Modes:**
- First-floor collapse
- Permanent lean
- Connection failures
- Complete building loss

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I call an engineer even if my building wasn't officially tagged?
Yes. Official rapid assessments may miss damage or provide only cursory evaluation. A professional engineering assessment provides detailed documentation valuable for insurance claims and identifies damage that may not be immediately obvious.

### How soon after an earthquake should I get assessment?
For safety concerns, immediately. For documentation, as soon as practical—ideally within days to weeks while damage is still clearly earthquake-related. Waiting too long can make it harder to link damage to the specific event.

### Will earthquake insurance cover engineering assessment costs?
Most earthquake policies cover reasonable costs for professional damage assessment as part of the claims process. Check your specific policy. We can provide documentation in formats that support claims submission.

### What's the difference between city inspection and engineering assessment?
City inspections (ATC-20) are rapid safety evaluations determining occupancy status. Engineering assessments are detailed professional evaluations documenting damage, determining causes, and supporting repair or insurance needs. They serve different purposes.

### Can earthquake-damaged buildings be repaired, or do they need replacement?
Most earthquake-damaged buildings can be repaired. The question is economic—whether repair cost justifies the investment versus replacement. Our assessment helps you understand damage extent and make informed decisions about repair versus replacement.

### How does liquefaction damage differ from shaking damage?
Shaking damage results from building response to ground motion. Liquefaction damage results from ground failure—soil losing strength and behaving like liquid. Liquefaction causes settlement, tilting, and lateral spreading. Different damage types require different repair approaches.

### Should I retrofit after repairs, or just restore to original condition?
This depends on your goals and building condition. We recommend considering strengthening beyond original during repairs—it's more cost-effective than separate retrofit later. We can assess vulnerability and recommend appropriate strengthening.

### What records should I keep after an earthquake?
Document everything: photographs of damage, official notices, contractor estimates, repair invoices, engineering reports, insurance correspondence. This documentation is valuable for claims, future sales, and demonstrating proper repair.
