## What Are Key Grading and Drainage Considerations?

**Cut and Fill Balance**
Efficient grading minimizes earthwork by balancing cut (excavation) with fill (placement) on-site. Importing or exporting soil adds significant cost.

**Slope Design**
Graded slopes must be stable:

- **Maximum Slopes:** Typically 2:1 (horizontal:vertical) for fill, 1.5:1 for cut in good soils.
- **Setbacks:** Buildings and improvements need adequate setback from slope crests and toes.
- **Drainage:** Slopes need interceptor drains above and drainage away at toe.

**Building Pad Grades**
Pad elevations consider:

- **Finished Floor Elevation:** Height relative to surrounding grades.
- **Drainage Away:** Minimum 2% slope away from buildings.
- **Access Grades:** Driveway connections to streets.
- **Flood Considerations:** Elevation above flood levels if applicable.

**Surface Drainage**
Proper surface drainage includes:

- **Minimum Slopes:** 2% on impervious surfaces, 3% on turf areas.
- **Flow Direction:** Always away from buildings toward collection points.
- **Swales:** Shallow channels directing flow without erosion.
- **Area Drains:** Collection points at low areas.

## What San Jacinto Grading Requirements Apply?

**Grading Permits**
Riverside County requires grading permits for earthwork exceeding threshold quantities (typically 50 cubic yards or more). Permits require:

- Engineered grading plans
- Erosion control measures
- Inspection during construction
- Certification upon completion

**Drainage Requirements**
Grading must not adversely affect neighboring properties:

- Historic drainage patterns generally must be maintained
- Concentrated flows must be properly managed
- Water quality considerations may apply

**Slope Setbacks**
Buildings typically require setbacks from slope crests and toes based on slope height and steepness.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need grading engineering for a single-family home in San Jacinto?**
Yes. Building permits require grading plans showing building pad elevation, drainage patterns, and earthwork quantities.

**How do I know if my site needs significant grading?**
Sites with substantial slope, elevation differences from street, or drainage challenges typically need more extensive grading. We evaluate conditions during consultation.

**What is pad certification and when is it required?**
Pad certification is an engineer's confirmation that the building pad was constructed per approved plans. Required before foundation construction for most graded sites.

**Can grading solve flooding problems on my property?**
Often yes. Grading can redirect drainage, establish proper slopes, and create swales that direct water away from problem areas.

**How long does grading engineering take?**
Typically 3-4 weeks for engineering, plus 3-4 weeks for Riverside County permit review.
